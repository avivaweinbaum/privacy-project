    Why knowing about Terms and Conditions/Privacy Policies are important:

    Terms and conditions act as a binding contract between the user and service while privacy policies demonstrate how user's personal information is being handled. In both circumstances, it is important to understand the information you are agreeing to. Understanding these allows users to have more control over their privacy and be able to make more informed decisions about which products to use. More sources: makeuseof.com

    How to find Terms and Conditions/Privacy Policies:

    Terms and conditions are not required by law, but privacy policies are. Almost all laws, such as the CalOPPA (California Online Privacy Protection Act), General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR, in Europe), and PIPEDA (Personal Information Protection and Electronic Documents Act) require privacy policies to be easy to find. The most common place a privacy policy can be found is in the footer of a webpage. Sometimes users will encounter terms and conditions on sign up forms or on checkout or sign in pages. If a user can not find the privacy policy in any of these places, the website is probably shady. Source: freeprivacypolicy.com

    Summaries of common key terms:

    Once you have read the definition of the key words, we recommend going back and re-reading the sentences where they are highlighted to get a better understanding of what is being said.

    Advertisers/Advertisments: Advertisers buy space from mobile publishers or ad networks to spread a message about their product. Advertisers often collect analytics about their website and advertisements in order to improve their marketing.

    Affiliates: Affiliates advertise another companyâ€™s products or services on their website. If you click on an advertisement from an affiliate, the link will put cookies in your browser to track your clicks and commission the affiliate if you buy anything.

    Automatic: Specifies the processes that occur automatically as you use this program.

    Consent: Under the GDPR, consent is defined as "any freely given, specific, informed and unambiguous clear affirmative action" by which a person gives permission for their personal data to be processed in a particular way. Companies should ask for consent when using cookies, sending marketing emails, or sharing personal data with other companies. Unfortunately, the GDPR does not apply in many countries.

    Cookies: A cookie collects information about users actions on websites. They help websites personalize information for users.

    Data: Privacy policies should specify what type of data is being collected.

    Discretion: States what areas the website expects users to exercise discretion.

    Information: Privacy policies should express exactly what type of information is being collected and for what purposes said information is being collected.

    Liable/Liability: Specifies what the website or user can or can not be held responsible for

    Location: Website and apps are required to clearly express if user location data is being tracked, especially if they continue to collect such data even after the user has closed the website or app. Users should have the option to turn tracking services off.

    Party/Parties/Party's: Businesses exchange data with third party services for many reasons, such as to build customer engagement and revenue. The third party may only collect data from the website to enhance its business but it may also have the rights to sell it.

    Protect: Websites and apps are often required to protect user data to some extent.

    Renewal: States if a subscription or any type of contract will be automatically renewed after a certain period of time.

    Responsible: The company may be specifying what the user is or is not accountable, or what they (the company) are or are not accountable.

    Third: Businesses exchange data with third party services for many reasons, such as to build customer engagement and revenue. The third party may only collect data from the website to enhance its business but it may also have the rights to sell it.

    Third Parties/Affiliates/Advertisers: The product may be giving or selling your data to third parties/affiliates/advertisers without requiring your consent each time, or may be restricting third parties from accessing your data.

    Track/Tracking: Website and apps are required to clearly express if user location data is being tracked, especially if they continue to collect such data even after the user has closed the website or app. Users should have the option to turn tracking services off.
